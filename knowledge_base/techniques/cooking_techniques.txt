=== COOKING TECHNIQUES KNOWLEDGE BASE ===

--- TECHNIQUE: Sautéing / Bhunna ---
Type: Dry-heat cooking
Best For: Onions, garlic, spices, vegetables, paneer
Temperature: Medium to high heat

Steps:
1. Heat pan until hot before adding oil
2. Add oil - it should shimmer but not smoke
3. Add ingredients - don't overcrowd the pan
4. Keep stirring or tossing
5. Listen for the sizzle - quiet sizzle means pan is too cold

Indian technique (Bhunna): Cook masala on high heat, stirring constantly, until oil separates from the paste. This indicates spices are fully cooked. 5-8 minutes of bhunna creates the base of most Indian curries.

Tips:
- Use wide, flat pan for more surface contact
- Dry vegetables before sautéing to prevent steaming
- Add aromatics (garlic, ginger) after onions, not before

--- TECHNIQUE: Tempering / Tadka (Chaunk) ---
Type: Indian flavor-building technique
Essential For: Dals, raita, chaas, chutneys, rice dishes

How to:
1. Heat oil or ghee in small pan on medium-high heat
2. Test heat by adding one mustard seed - it should pop immediately
3. Add seeds in order: mustard → cumin → dried red chilies → curry leaves → onions
4. When mustard seeds stop popping and cumin turns golden, pour over cooked dish immediately
5. Cover immediately to trap aroma

Common tempering oils: Ghee (North India), Coconut oil (South India), Mustard oil (Bengal), Vegetable oil (neutral)

Regional variations:
- North Indian: Ghee + cumin + ginger-garlic
- South Indian: Coconut oil + mustard + curry leaves + dried red chili + urad dal
- Bengali: Mustard oil + panch phoron
- Gujarati: Oil + mustard + asafoetida + turmeric

--- TECHNIQUE: Pressure Cooking ---
Type: Wet-heat, fast cooking
Best For: Lentils, beans, rice, tough vegetables, meats
Key Benefit: Cuts cooking time by 60-70%

Guidelines:
- Lentils (dal): 2-3 whistles (5-7 minutes)
- Chickpeas (soaked): 5-6 whistles (15 minutes)
- Rajma/kidney beans (soaked): 6-7 whistles (20 minutes)
- Rice: 2 whistles (5-6 minutes)
- Potatoes: 2-3 whistles

Safety rules:
- Never fill pressure cooker more than 2/3 full
- Always use required liquid (minimum 1 cup)
- Don't open immediately - wait for pressure to release
- Check rubber gasket and vent before every use

--- TECHNIQUE: Dum Cooking ---
Type: Slow steam cooking
Best For: Biryani, korma, slow-cooked curries
Origin: Mughal cuisine

How to:
1. Partially cook main ingredients (rice 70%, meat/vegetables 80%)
2. Layer ingredients in heavy-bottomed pot
3. Seal pot with dough or foil to trap steam
4. Cook on very low heat using:
   a. Indirect heat (heavy griddle/tava under pot)
   b. Hot charcoal on lid (traditional dum)
5. Let steam work for 20-30 minutes

Tips: The trapped steam finishes cooking while flavors meld together. Do not open mid-cooking.

--- TECHNIQUE: Blanching Vegetables ---
Type: Quick boiling + ice bath
Best For: Preserving color, texture, nutritional value
Essential For: Spinach (for palak dishes), green beans, broccoli

Steps:
1. Bring large pot of salted water to rapid boil
2. Add vegetables - cook briefly (30 seconds for spinach, 2 minutes for broccoli)
3. Immediately transfer to bowl of ice water (equal parts ice and water)
4. Leave in ice water 2-3 minutes
5. Drain well before using

Why it works: Blanching deactivates enzymes that cause color and nutrient loss. Ice bath stops cooking immediately.

--- TECHNIQUE: Caramelizing Onions ---
Type: Slow dry heat
Time Required: 30-45 minutes (do not rush)
Best For: Biryani fried onions, French onion soup, curry base

Steps:
1. Slice onions thinly and evenly
2. Heat pan with oil + butter on medium-low heat
3. Add onions with pinch of salt (draws out moisture)
4. Stir every 5 minutes
5. Cook until deep golden-amber (not brown/burnt)

Common mistake: Turning heat high to speed up - this burns outside before inside cooks. Patience is key.

--- TECHNIQUE: Making Roti/Chapati ---
Type: Dry griddle cooking
Essential Knowledge: Indian bread making

Dough preparation:
- Ratio: 2 cups whole wheat flour + 3/4 cup water (approximately)
- Knead for 8-10 minutes until smooth and elastic
- Rest covered for 30 minutes (very important for soft rotis)
- Dough should be soft but not sticky

Rolling:
- Portion into golf-ball sized pieces
- Dust with flour, roll to even circle (6-7 inch diameter)
- Aim for even thickness throughout

Cooking on tawa:
1. Heat iron tawa on high heat (very hot)
2. Place roti, cook until bubbles form (30-40 seconds)
3. Flip, cook second side until brown spots appear
4. Optionally puff directly on flame
5. Apply ghee immediately

Signs of perfect roti: Even brown spots, puffs up, soft and pliable

--- TECHNIQUE: Deep Frying ---
Type: Hot oil cooking
Best For: Pakoras, samosas, puri, bhatura
Safety: High risk technique

Temperature guidelines:
- Test with wooden chopstick: bubbles should form immediately
- Ideal temperature: 350-375°F (175-190°C)
- Use thermometer for accuracy

Safety rules:
1. Never leave hot oil unattended
2. Dry foods before frying (water causes splatter)
3. Don't overcrowd (drops temperature)
4. Use deep, heavy-bottomed pan
5. Have lid nearby for fire safety
6. Never pour water on oil fire - use baking soda or lid

--- TECHNIQUE: Making Indian Masala Base ---
The foundation of almost all Indian curries

Ratio (for 4 servings):
- 2 medium onions
- 2 medium tomatoes  
- 1 teaspoon ginger-garlic paste
- Base spices: turmeric, coriander powder, cumin powder, red chili powder

Steps:
1. Finely chop or blend onions for smoother curry
2. Cook onions until golden (10-12 minutes) - this is crucial, don't rush
3. Add ginger-garlic paste, cook 2 minutes
4. Add tomatoes, cook until mushy
5. Add ground spices, cook until oil separates (5 minutes)
6. This is your masala base - add protein/vegetables here

The oil separating from masala is the most important sign that the base is properly cooked.

--- TECHNIQUE: Knife Skills ---
Basic cuts for Indian cooking:

Fine chop (for onions in curry): Thin, even pieces cook uniformly and create smooth sauce
Rough chop (for blending): Larger pieces, will be blended anyway
Julienne (for stir-fry): Thin matchsticks cook quickly
Dicing (for curries): Even cubes for consistent cooking

Safety:
- Always curl fingers when cutting (claw grip)
- Keep knife sharp (dull knives are more dangerous)
- Cut on stable cutting board (wet towel underneath prevents slipping)

--- TECHNIQUE: Storage and Preservation ---

Fresh vegetables:
- Root vegetables (potatoes, onions): Cool, dark, dry place - NOT refrigerator
- Leafy greens: Refrigerate wrapped in damp paper towel
- Tomatoes: Room temperature until ripe, then refrigerate
- Fresh herbs: Stand in glass of water like flowers, refrigerate

Cooked food:
- Refrigerate within 2 hours of cooking
- Store in airtight containers
- Cool completely before refrigerating (don't put hot food in fridge)
- Label with date

Spices:
- Store in airtight containers away from heat, light, and moisture
- Whole spices last longer than ground (2 years vs 6 months)
- Buy small quantities for maximum freshness
- Smell test: if no aroma, time to replace

--- TECHNIQUE: Cooking Rice Perfectly ---
Absorption Method (most common):
- Ratio: 1 cup rice : 1.5-2 cups water
- Rinse rice until water runs clear (removes excess starch)
- Bring water to boil, add salt
- Add rice, bring to boil again
- Reduce to low simmer, cover tightly
- Cook 15-18 minutes (white rice), 40-45 minutes (brown rice)
- Remove from heat, let rest 10 minutes without opening lid
- Fluff with fork before serving

Mistakes to avoid:
- Don't lift lid during cooking
- Don't stir (makes it gluey)
- Let it rest after cooking

Basmati rice:
- Soak 30 minutes before cooking for extra fluffy, separate grains
- Use 1:1.5 ratio
- Rinse gently to avoid breaking grains
